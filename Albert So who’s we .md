Albert stood in the kitchen with one hand against the counter, slowly filling his coffee mug while
the call continued through the speaker of his phone. 
The legal advisor, Mike, had called regarding the mobile safety framework tied to TRIAD-NET — the 
same evolving system Albert had buried himself inside for months. The conversation had gone far beyond ordinary app agreements. 
Mike had expected a rough concept, maybe an unfinished prototype with optimistic language around public safety. Instead, Albert
walked him through node agreements, distributed relay systems, anonymous user classifications, map-event logic, and layered legal
safeguards surrounding live GPS interaction between cyclists, pedestrians, vehicles, and passengers.

Mike was visibly impressed even through the audio compression of the call. Every question he asked was answered almost immediately,
as if Albert had already simulated every possible legal objection weeks earlier. Liability trees. Delayed signal handling.
User consent structures. Event verification thresholds. Albert explained them one after another with the same unsettling precision 
he always carried when his mind entered full motion.

“Yeah,” Albert finally said while lifting the coffee mug from beneath the machine, “we really put a lot of time into this project.
Pretty much mapped out every direction of outcomes.”

There was a brief silence on the other end before Mike gave a low laugh of disbelief.

“Well,” Mike admitted, “I’ll tell you right now, most startups don’t think this far ahead. Especially not for something this ambitious.”

The conversation continued another fifteen minutes. They discussed future legal exposure if TRIAD-NET ever scaled nationally,
especially if municipalities or emergency systems became involved. Mike reminded Albert that anything operating on live safety
prediction maps would have to be legally precise down to the smallest function. Albert agreed quietly. He already knew.

When the call finally ended, Albert stood motionless for a few seconds staring into the dark reflection inside the coffee. 
The kitchen remained dim except for the low light above the stove.

Mia had been sitting nearby the entire time.

She looked at him carefully.

“So who’s we?”

Albert glanced up.

“You’re in your office day and night since your friend’s death,” she continued softly. “You barely sleep. 
You come out for coffee and go right back in there. Even if you two weren’t close anymore, 
I know he mattered to you. But you have to move on eventually.” She paused. “You’re never on the phone with anyone… so who’s ‘we’?”

Albert frowned immediately.

That question hit harder than the legal discussion had.

Because now he had a choice.

He could lie.

Not a major lie — at least not by normal standards. Something harmless. 
Maybe say he meant developers online, old archived notes, collaborative planning documents.
He could even disguise it with humor. But Albert hated lying to Mia. Even small lies stayed
in his mind like structural flaws inside an equation. And jokes never worked on her anyway. She dismantled them too quickly.

An uncomfortable silence settled over the kitchen.

Albert’s thoughts accelerated the way they always did — branching possibilities at impossible speed, 
entire conversational outcomes mapping themselves forward in his head before either of them spoke again.

Finally, reluctantly, Albert answered.

“The AI on the computer.”

Mia stared at him.

For one second her jaw actually dropped.

Then she smiled.

Then she laughed.

“That’s hilarious.”

Albert didn’t laugh.

Mia shook her head, still smiling now more out of relief than amusement.

“No, seriously,” she said, “I was worried you meant some secret business partner or something.” 
She leaned against the counter. “But honestly? A road safety app like TRIAD-NET would have to be
scaled perfectly in every virtue imaginable. If you’re using live GPS maps for people’s safety,
it would have to be legally precise down to the core functions.” She laughed again softly. 
“And if it actually works, they’ll know exactly where to find you.”

Albert lowered his eyes toward the coffee mug.

That was exactly what bothered him.

He had become increasingly reluctant to present anything publicly. The deeper TRIAD-NET evolved,
the more he understood the implications of a system capable of predicting dangerous path intersections 
seconds before impact. A network where cyclists, pedestrians, passengers, and vehicles could all receive synchronized proximity 
alerts before crossing trajectories. It sounded simple when spoken aloud. But underneath it existed a massive structure of legal
logic, mathematical weighting, timing systems, node trust relationships, and human unpredictability.

And underneath all of that was grief.

His childhood friend’s death had never really left him.

Even if they had gone separate ways years ago, the accident stayed embedded in Albert’s mind like an unresolved variable. 
The realization haunted him constantly: a few seconds of warning might have changed everything.

That single thought had eventually become TRIAD-NET.

Not fame.

Not money.

Not even invention.

Just the obsessive idea that somewhere between signal delay, map prediction, and human movement there might exist a 
narrow mathematical window where catastrophe could still be avoided.

Mia studied him quietly now, her expression softening as she finally realized he was serious.

“You really think this could save people, don’t you?”

Albert looked toward the dark hallway leading back to his office.

The monitors inside still glowed faintly through the doorway.

“…Yeah,” he answered quietly. “I do.”

The room fell silent again except for the low ticking of the kitchen clock.

And somewhere inside the office beyond the hallway, the TRIAD-NET simulations continued running across the screens —
anonymous nodes moving endlessly across glowing maps, thousands of possible collisions appearing and disappearing before they could ever happen.
