TRIAD-NET: The Orange Driver

Five years had passed since Albert Madsen introduced TRIAD-NET to the public.

What had begun as a mobile safety application designed to reduce roadway accidents had grown to reach international clients. 
TRIAD-NET was a predictive transportation network that connected vehicles, cyclists, pedestrians, passengers, roadway sensors,
satellite imaging systems, and government traffic infrastructure into a single safety platform. The system's purpose was simple: 
identify dangerous situations before they became accidents.

Most users only knew TRIAD-NET as the navigation application sitting on their dashboard installed in the latest model cars as a 
navigation map touch screen, or with mobile app with car mount   on cell phone (Both voice guidance default on/off button. 
The software provided directions, traffic alerts, safety notifications, and predictive warnings while they traveled.
Few people realized that behind the familiar moving map display existed an enormous motherboard network constantly 
analyzing roadway conditions in real time.

Over the years Albert had continued improving the system. New user node agreements allowed additional safety monitoring
while maintaining user anonymity. Drivers were never identified by name. Instead they existed as anonymous nodes moving 
throughout the transportation network by user's login agreement as vehicle, passenger, cyclist, passenger or pedestrian. 
The motherboard observed behavior rather than identity.

The latest version introduced a status system.

Most users remained Status White. White Status indicated a normal driver operating within roadway laws and safety regulations.

Drivers who displayed unusual behavior patterns, frequent near-misses, or developing risk indicators became Status Blue. 
Blue Status served as an early warning category and did not necessarily indicate illegal activity.

Drivers who repeatedly violated traffic laws accumulated strikes within the TRIAD-NET system. Strikes were issued only when an actual traffic
law had been broken.

After fifteen confirmed traffic-law violations within a review period, a driver automatically triggered Orange Status.
The mobile application would begin displaying warning notifications while nearby users received alerts regarding repeated 
illegal driving activity in the area. Orange Status remained active until the end of each month or billing period cycle.

The state government quickly recognized the value of the technology. Years of roadway accident data revealed patterns nobody 
had previously noticed. Dangerous intersections, poorly designed turns, hidden visibility problems, and confusing traffic
layouts became visible through TRIAD-NET's historical records. Road crews redesigned troublesome locations while infrastructure budgets expanded.

With government support came larger systems. Larger systems required larger computers.

Soon Albert found himself sitting in front of a massive Xeon 6980 workstation that resembled something between a 
traffic control center and a science-fiction command deck.

The newest TRIAD-NET upgrades could utilize satellite image feeds to monitor traffic conditions. On clear
days the system could identify small vehicles and even distinguish color patterns. Sensors interacted with 
stop signs, stoplights, lane markings, roadway construction zones, and traffic flow patterns. Every piece of 
information flowed back into the motherboard.

The simulation software uses two prediction engines responsible for much of this work was as one engine
performed Physics between users the other did the math on alerts and notification in respsonse.

Two separate specialized in behavioral forecasting.physics simulation library specialized in physics calculations.
Together they could estimate vehicle movement, traffic conflicts, collision probabilities, braking distances,
and accident risks before they occurred.Albert often compared the system to air traffic control. Pilots could not see every aircraft at once. Controllers 
could. The same concept applied to roadways. TRIAD-NET watched the entire picture.

One evening Albert sat alone in front of the workstation reviewing anonymous user activity across several live maps.
Thousands of white icons moved through cities and highways. Most represented ordinary drivers heading home after work.
A handful of Blue Status users appeared.
A few Orange users appeared.
Nothing unusual.
Yet recently Albert had become fascinated with one particular Orange Status driver.
The driver seemed to appear everywhere.
No matter what update Albert released, the same user somehow found a new way to trigger alerts.
At first Albert suspected something serious.
Then something strange happened.
He began dreaming about the driver.
The dreams always started the same way.
Late at night.
A sports car.
An engine roaring somewhere behind him.
Bright headlights flashing in his rear view mirror.
The image reminded Albert of an actual encounter weeks earlier when TRIAD-NET had issued an illegal driver warning 
in the area while he was driving home from work.
A sports car had appeared behind him moments later.
The driver repeatedly flashed the headlights.
The engine screamed.
The vehicle swerved into another lane.
Albert remembered thinking the maneuver would trigger another strike.
The driver was already well over the Orange threshold.
The memory stayed with him.
The next morning Albert woke with a new idea.

Perhaps TRIAD-NET should expand beyond Orange Status.
Perhaps the system should automatically report severe repeat offenders.
Perhaps it should temporarily suspend users from certain network privileges.
The thoughts lingered.
Yet something else lingered too.
The image of that sports car.
It felt as though the mystery was calling him back toward the workstation.
If the driver existed, Albert thought, then eventually he could identify the pattern.
He began taking notes.
Around the same time TRIAD-NET entered a new rehabilitation program for DUI offenders. Vehicles participating 
in the program contained breathalyzer systems integrated directly into TRIAD-NET's navigation software. 
The system monitored compliance and driving behavior as part of rehabilitation efforts.
That is when Albert encountered the Orange Driver again.
At first he did not know the man's name.
He only knew the statistics.

Every evening the workstation generated the same notification.
Orange Driver Detected.
Somewhere on the map an icon would suddenly accelerate as though it had just remembered an emergency.
Albert would zoom in.
The icon would miss a stop sign.
The icon would brake too late.
The icon would make a questionable turn.
The icon would challenge a yellow light and lose.

Again.
Again.
Again.

Eventually the workstation seemed more interested in the vehicle than Albert was.
One evening Physics and alert engines generated a prediction.
Probability driver will miss next yellow light: 94%.
The driver missed it.
Albert stared at the screen."How?"
The workstation recalculated.
Unknown. User appears committed.
Weeks passed.
Albert started reviewing historical route logs.
That is when the mystery became comedy.
One morning the driver spent twenty minutes searching for sunglasses that were already on his head.
TRIAD-NET did not issue a strike because no law had been broken, but the workstation generated a behavioral note and archived the incident anyway.
Another day he managed to lock himself out of his own car while holding the keys. Again, no strike was issued. 
The workstation simply generated an automated note.
User successfully secured vehicle from himself.
Albert laughed so hard he nearly spilled coffee onto the keyboard.

Another day the driver left home and forgot his wallet. He returned. Then forgot his phone. He returned again.
Then forgot why he returned. TRIAD-NET recorded the unusual route activity but issued no strikes because no traffic laws had been violated.
Albert stared at the route map.
The path resembled a confused squirrel attempting advanced mathematics.
Eventually the driver's identity became available through rehabilitation records.
His name was Guss.
Everything suddenly made sense.
Guss drove an aging sports car with a custom exhaust system loud enough to announce itself three counties away.
He wore a baseball cap, sported a smoker's beard, blasted rock music through worn speakers, 
and possessed the unique ability to transform ordinary driving into a statistical anomaly.

The vehicle had horsepower.
The vehicle had torque.
The vehicle had absolutely no chance of making Guss arrive on time.
That was another discovery.
Guss was always late.
Late to work.
Late to appointments.
Late to band practice.
Late to cookouts.
Late to traffic safety classes.
Especially traffic safety classes.

"Slow down."

"Potential hazard ahead."

Albert burst out laughing.
For years he had been hunting a villain.
Instead he had discovered a man accidentally treating roadway safety like a competitive sport.

Engineers suspected a software malfunction.

Albert knew better.

By understanding Guss.
Years later engineers still asked Albert about the strangest driver TRIAD-NET had ever encountered.
Albert would smile.
Then point toward an archived file labeled:

ORANGE DRIVER #1
Inside existed thousands of pages of violations, reroutes, missed turns, impossible decisions,
arguments with navigation software, behavioral incidents, and enough accidental comedy to keep programmers entertained for days.
Somewhere out on the highway Guss still drove his sports car.
Rock music blasted through the speakers.
The exhaust roared.
The engine growled.
And Guss proudly obeyed the speed limit by exactly one mile per hour.
Albert leaned back in his chair and shut down the workstation.
The mystery was solved.
The villain never existed.
Only Guss.

The only man in state history capable of turning a traffic safety network, a DUI rehabilitation program, 
a breathalyzer system, a satellite-assisted navigation platform, an artificial intelligence prediction engine,
and a government-funded super workstation into a years-long comedy.

For the first time in a very long time, Albert laughed harder than the programmers.

Then he went home.
